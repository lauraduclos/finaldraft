Opioid Rescue Kits Hidden in Plain Sight of New Yorkers

By Laura Duclos

In 2016, opioids were involved in 44% of overdose deaths in 2016, a 28% increase from 2015, according to the NYC Department of Health. Fentanyl, a synthetic and fast-acting opiate is the face of the opioid epidemic and most commonly present in heroin and cocaine-involved deaths. The drug is known to have potency 50 to 100 times the strength of morphine.

Despite the rising trend of fentanyl-related deaths, drug users are still seeking new ways to combine the opioids with other drugs, causing dangerous and sometimes lethal reactions. "What people are doing now is giving themselves a double dose," David Cornish, a field coordinator with Harlem United, a harm reduction center in Manhattan, said.

"A lot of clients are on methadone, and when they are on methadone they're more likely to use heroin, which could be laced with fentanyl. And the fentanyl and methadone together is like a deadly dosage," said Cornish.

According to the New York Health Department website, the government has created public service announcements, created plans and spent thousands towards community center-based programs like Harlem United and for naloxone, the antidote administered when a person has overdosed on opioids and prescription painkillers.

Community programs also work to educate the public on safer drug intake, safe sex and disease prevention.

Along with these items, community centers give out Naloxone, commonly known as Narcan, to people who may be at risk of an overdose, or have loved ones who are addicted to opiods.

Narcan is the antidote to fentanyl. Centers offer training for the antidote, involving methods to recognize the signs of an overdose and how to distribute the medicine, at no cost.

Harlem United, which was founded over three decades ago in the height of the AIDS and HIV crisis, is just one of several nonprofit harm reduction centers in the city that places itself on the frontline in the battle of the nationally-spread opioid epidemic.

“When we have somebody overdose, most of the time it’s from fentanyl.”

Billy Garcia, a case manager at Washington Heights Corner Project, a harm reduction center located closer to the Bronx, sees opioid overdoses increasing at an unsettling pace. He works with opioid users on a daily basis, in efforts to help them maintain and eventually abandon their addiction.

Garcia said one of the leading factors in opioid’s rising popularity is cost.

“Fentanyl has changed the game big time up here,” Garcia said. “Before, there were $10 bags. Now there’s $5 bags because people put fentanyl in them.”

But his mentality towards halting the epidemic is contrary to what most would think logical.

The idea of Garcia and many community centers is to assist people in their addiction and not cut it out of users’ lives completely.

“There’s people who need help,” Garcia said. “Just because somebody does drugs doesn’t mean they’re not human. They need to have a place where people actually care for them."

As backward-minded as helping people do drugs may sound, New York State government agrees.

In 2015, the State government made it legal for all residents of New York to carry and administer overdose rescue kits that contain naloxone. There are over 2,000 pharmacies in the state that carry naloxone.

740 of those are in the city alone.

Despite the efforts, fewer people than originally hoped have been trained in and carry rescue kits and community centers fear it may be for stigma.

“I feel like everyone in some way or another does drugs – it could be legal like alcohol,” Garcia said. “Anybody could lose control of their use. Anyone could overindulge and there needs to people to help them. They need to teach them to not overindulge and drink in moderation and I feel like it’s the same thing for drugs.”

Many harm reduction centers are in upper Manhattan and the Bronx in order to be easily accessible.

According to a 2017 report from the Office of the Mayor, four neighborhoods in the Bronx had the highest rates of opioid overdose in 2015 and 2016. The report also stated police passed out over 1,000 naloxone kits last year. However, there are about 140 pharmacies in the Bronx alone that readily distribute naloxone kits to the public.
